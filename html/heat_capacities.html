Heat Capacities

EoS Approach

General

According to Haile and O'Connell (2005), heat capacities are sensitive to changes in T, generally they increase with increasing T. But, except near the gas-liquid critical point, they are weak functions of P and V. This allows us to treat capacities $C_p$ and $C_v$ mainly as the function of $T$: $C_p = C_p(T)$ and $C_v = C_v(T)$

Let we are provided with a general cubic equation of state given in the form \[ P = \frac{RT}{V - b} - \frac{a(T)}{V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2} \] with a coupled compressibility factor equation being \[ Z^3 + (\nu B - B - 1)Z^2 + (A + \mu B^2 - \nu B - \nu B^2) Z - (AB + \mu B^2 + \mu B^3) = 0 \] where $A = a(T) P \left/ R^2 T^2 \right.$ and $B = bP \left/ RT \right.$.

There are well known identities: \[ C_v - C_p = T \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)^2_P \left/ \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial P}\right)_T \right. = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)^2_V \left/ \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_T \right. \] Using equation of state, we could reduce the computation of $C_v$ to the computation of $C_p$ as follows.

\[ \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{R}{V - b} - \frac{a'(T)}{V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2} \] \[ \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_T = \frac{a(T)(2V + \nu b)}{(V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2)^2} - \frac{RT}{(V - b)^2} \]

\[ C_v - C_p = T\frac{\left[R (V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2) - a'(T)(V - b)\right]^2} {a(T)(2V + \nu b)(V - b)^2 - RT(V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2)^2} \]

Now, using corresponding states principle we could split our isobaric heat capacity $C_p$ into reference value $C_p^0$ computed for ideal gas and departure function $C_p^{\text{res}}$: \[ C_p = C_p^0 + C_p^{\text{res}} \] Our task now is to compute both summands.

Ideal Gas

Big advantage of the ideal gas setting is the independence of its parameters. We could express isobaric heat capacity of the mixture through the capacities of its components: \[ C_p^0 = \sum_{i=1}^N z_i C^0_{p, i} \]

There are several methods for estimating isobaric heat capacity of the simple fluid. We will mention Joback method and TRC polynomial equation for $C^0_p /R$. We first describe Joback method and then TRC polynomial equation.

The Joback method described in section 3-3 of Poling et. al (2001) or in section 6-3 of Reid et. al (1987). This is the contributional method, i.e. it forms the required capacity counting different parts of molecular structure like —CH3, —COOH, etc., so it is good for the cases of simple fluid with only only chemical formula known.

Main idea that $C_p^0$ being primary the function of temperature is approximated as \[ C_p^0(T) = A + BT + CT^2 + DT^3 \] where $A$, $B$, $C$ and $D$ depend on the structure of the molecule and computed as follows:

$$A = \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \Delta_a - 37.93$$ $$B = \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \Delta_b + 0.210$$
$$C = \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \Delta_c - 3.91 \times 10^{-4}$$ $$D = \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \Delta_d + 2.06 \times 10^{-7}$$

Corresponding deltas values could be found in table 6-1 of Reid et. al (1987) or table C-1 of Poling et. al (2001).

TRC polynomial equation is due to Thermodynamics Research Center at College Station, TX, USA. It also provides a polynomial representation for $C_p^0$ as the function of temperature \[ \frac{C_p^0}{R} = a_0 + a_1 T + a_2 T^2 + a_3 T^3 + a_4 T^4 \] using coefficients, provided by TRC.

Coefficients $a_0, \dots, a_4$ could be found in table A section C of Poling et. al (2001). Some values for liquid and vapor heat capacities for T = 298.15K are presented ibid.

Departure Function

Once we provided with analytic equation of state, we could derive analytic expression for departure function of isobaric heat capacity $C_p^0$. Derivations for general form of cubic equation of state and its specifications for Peng-Robinson and Soave-Redlich-Kwong equations are given in Chung-Nin Mak thesis. Some of the formulas are represented below.

Suppose we are provided with a general cubic equation of state given in the form: \[ P = \frac{RT}{V - b} - \frac{a(T)}{V^2 + \nu b V + \mu b^2} \] with couple compressibility factor equation being \[ Z^3 + (\nu B - B - 1)Z^2 + (A + \mu B^2 - \nu B - \nu B^2) Z - (AB + \mu B^2 + \mu B^3) = 0 \] where $A = \frac{a(T) P}{R^2 T^2}$ and $B = \frac{bP}{RT}$.

Then the departure function for isobaric heat capacity could be expressed as \[ C_p - C_p^0 = \frac{R(M - N)^2}{M^2 - A(2Z + \nu B)} - a''(T) \frac{T}{2\theta_1b} \ln \left\{\frac{Z + \theta_2 B}{Z + \theta_3 B}\right\} - R \] where

$$M = \frac{Z^2 + \nu BZ + \mu B^2}{Z - B}$$ $$N = a'(T) \frac{B}{Rb}$$
$$\theta_1 = \sqrt{\nu^2/4 - \mu}$$ $$\theta_2 = \nu/2 - \theta_1$$ $$\theta_3 = \nu/2 + \theta_1$$

Heat Capacities of Liquids

Derivation from Enthalpy

By definition \[ C_p = \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial T}\right)_P \] Since we are provided with equation of state, we need to derive expression for enthalpy $H$ which we could differentiate. The following derivations follow those of section 8.2 of Pedersen and Christensen (2006).

First of all we relate enthalpy of our real fluid with enthalpy of ideal gas: \[ H = H^{\text{ig}} + H^{\text{res}} \] In case of mixture enthalpy of ideal gas is the simple compound \[ H^{\text{ig}} = \sum_{i = 1}^N z_i H_i^{\text{ig}} \]

See section 6.6 in Poling et. al. (2001)
