Pressure Simulation

Let us consider some numeric toy example which would help understand the notion of pressure. Suppose we have one dimensional interval with some point masses on it. Initially we set masses, positions and velocities of points at random.

After inital conditions are prescribed, we start to evolve this dynamical system. We suppose that points collide as ellastic balls with each other and with walls. The impulse of force, which ball exert to the wall, equals to double absolute value of ball momentum. We would count such an impulses as compounds of our numeric pressure.

Obviously pressure would be proportional to the time system was simulated, so it make sense to consider normalized pressure $\frac{P}{T}$. What this normalized pressure would be proportional in terms of initial conditions? The possibilities are:

  • sum of masses $\sum m_i$
  • sum of absolute value of momentums $\sum m_i v_i$
  • sum of kenetic energies $\sum \frac{m_i v_i^2}2$

Accelerating the Portion of Fluid

Consider a simple setting. We are provided with some portion of heterogeneous fluid, which is initially at rest. We accelerate it with constant acceleration $a$ as time goes from $0$ to $1$. As a result, portion would move with speed $a$ and would possess kinetic energy $\frac{Ma^2}2$ where $M$ denotes total mass of the portion.

Consider this process as an adiabatic. Then work $\frac{Ma^2}2$ applied to the systems results in internal energy increase. We would try to look at this process locally and would make a number of assumptions.

  • our portion is located in "1D interval" with two point walls
  • molecules collide like elastic balls
  • molecule to wall collision is described by spring model

Provided that fluid is heterogeneous, all molecules have the same mass which we denote as $m$. Assumption about 1D location allows us to treat molecules collision as penetration. In other words, molecules pass each other as if they exist in different worlds.

Whats happening when molecule collides into wall? We assume that the wall acts as membrane and exert force on molecule proportional to the penetration of molecule. Consider two simple cases. Let us denote the stiffness of the wall with $k$.

Case #1: molecule goes "to the left" with velocity $-v$ and meets the wall at the origin at time moment $t = 0$. At the same moment wall starts to accelerate "to the right" with acceleration $a$. Then, if we denote position of molecule as $u(t)$ we have the following ODE for it: \begin{aligned} m \ddot{u} & = k \left[at^2/2 - u \right] \\ u(0) & = 0 \\ \dot{u}(0) & = -v \end{aligned}

Case #2: molecule goes "to the right" with velocity $v$ and meets the wall at the origin at time moment $t = 0$. At the same moment wall starts to accelerate "to the right" with acceleration $a$. Then, if we denote position of molecule as $u(t)$ we have the following ODE for it: \begin{aligned} m \ddot{u} & = - k \left[u - at^2/2 \right] \\ u(0) & = 0 \\ \dot{u}(0) & = v \end{aligned}

These two equations differ only at initial conditions: $\dot{u}(0) = \pm v$. Let us introduce $\alpha = \frac{k}{m}$. Then the system turns into: \begin{aligned} & \ddot{u} + \alpha u = \alpha at^2/2 \\ & u(0) = 0 \\ & \dot{u}(0) = \pm v \end{aligned}

General solution for such a second order ODE is the sum of the particular solution $u_1(t)$ and general solution for homogenized system $u_2(t)$ where \begin{aligned} u_1(t) & = \frac{a}2 t^2 - \frac{a}{\alpha} \\ u_2(t) & = c_0 \sin (\sqrt{\alpha} t) + c_1 \cos (\sqrt{\alpha} t) \end{aligned}

Solving for $c_0$ and $c_1$ from boundary conditions, we end up with the solution: \[ u(t) = \frac{a}2 t^2 + \frac{a}{\alpha} \left[\cos(\sqrt{\alpha} t) - 1 \right] \pm \frac{v}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t) \]

Time moment $t_0$ when molecule bounce off the wall is described by $u(t_0) = at^2_0 / 2$ or \[ \frac{a}{\alpha} \left[\cos(\sqrt{\alpha} t_0) - 1 \right] \pm \frac{v}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t_0) = 0 \] Let us denote $\sqrt{\alpha} t_0$ as $x_0$ then this is equivalent to \[ \mp \frac{a}{v \sqrt{\alpha}} = \frac{\sin(x_0)}{\cos(x_0) - 1} = - \frac{\cos(x_0 / 2)}{\sin(x_0 / 2)} \] and \[ t_0 = \pm \frac{2}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \arctan \left(\frac{v \sqrt{\alpha}}{a}\right) \]

Provided that, we could compute \begin{aligned} \sin(x_0) & = \pm \frac{2 \sqrt{\alpha} av}{a^2 + \alpha v^2} \\ \cos(x_0) & = \frac{a^2 - \alpha v^2}{a^2 + \alpha v^2} \end{aligned} and then velocity of molecule at the moment of separation is given by \[ \left.\dot{u}(t)\right|_{t_0} = \left.at - \frac{a}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t) \pm v \cos(\sqrt{\alpha} t) \right|_{t_0} = at_0 \mp v \]

Work of force, applied to the molecule, is expressed as \[ \int_{0}^{t_0} m \ddot{u} \cdot \dot{u} dt = \\ m \left(\frac{a^2t^2}2 + \frac{a^2 - \alpha v^2}{2\alpha} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t)^2 - \frac{a^2t}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t) + a t v \cos(\sqrt{\alpha} t) - \frac{a v}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \sin(\sqrt{\alpha} t)\cos(\sqrt{\alpha} t) \right|_0^{t_0} \]
